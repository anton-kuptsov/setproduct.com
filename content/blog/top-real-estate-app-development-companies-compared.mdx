## Why business models in real estate app development are different product architectures

Every real estate product type has a different definition of core features: 

-   For a rental marketplace, core features are search and discovery, listing management, tenant screening, lease generation, and payment processing. The product success is determined on how well it handles the transaction between landlord and tenant, and how reliably it processes recurring payments.
-   For a brokerage platform, the core is agent productivity: MLS/IDX data feeds, lead routing, CRM pipeline management, showing schedules, and offer tracking. The product needs to reduce friction in that relationship across a long, multi-touch sales cycle.
-   For a property management tool, the core shifts to operational reliability: maintenance request handling, vendor management, document storage, compliance tracking, and financial reporting across a portfolio.
-   For an investment or fractional ownership platform, the architecture centers on financial data accuracy, regulatory compliance, investor reporting, and in some cases secondary market infrastructure. The user is making capital allocation decisions, and the product needs to support that with data they can trust.

These differences drive decisions about data models, third-party integrations, notification systems, user roles, and performance requirements. A team that has built one type well is not automatically equipped to build another.

## What happens after vendor selection: The first weeks that define success

This chapter is a practical framework for executives and product owners who have selected a partner and need to know what to do next.

#### Week 1–2: Run a Structured Discovery Workshop

The first two weeks should be spent building a shared understanding of what you're building, why, and for whom — not writing code. The output isn't a slide deck; it's a living reference document that both sides can point to when scope debates emerge at week 11.

#### Week 2–4: Align on KPIs Before the Roadmap Is Set

By week four you should have agreed on the specific metrics that define success at 30, 60, and 90 days post-launch. KPIs set before the roadmap is locked carry real weight; KPIs defined after the fact are just post-hoc justification.

#### Week 3–5: Complete a Technical Audit of Existing Assets

A technical audit covers current codebase quality, documentation, third-party integration reliability, and licensing terms. The deliverable should be a written assessment with explicit flags for anything that will affect the timeline, architecture, or budget. 

#### Week 6–8: Establish Stakeholder Ownership and Review Protocols

Assign a single internal product owner with actual authority to approve designs, approve feature implementations, and make trade-off calls. Identify everyone else who needs to be consulted (e.g., legal, compliance, operations) and get their expected review turnaround in writing before the project depends on them.

#### Week 9: Review the Foundation Before It Gets Built On

Before the project moves into feature development, run a foundation review. This is a structured assessment of the technical and process decisions made in the first phase of the engagement.

The foundation review should cover: 

-   whether the architecture aligns with your performance and scalability requirements;
-   whether the data model supports the features planned for phases two and three;
-   whether the testing coverage is sufficient for a production real estate system;
-   whether the team communication and delivery cadence are working.

If anything in the foundation review surfaces a significant concern, address it now.
